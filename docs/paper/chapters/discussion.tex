\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This year's participation represents the most significant architectural evolution of our BioASQ pipeline since we began competing. Several themes emerge from our experience with the 14th edition.

\subsection{Impact of Architectural Changes}

The migration from PyTerrier PISA to pg\_textsearch for BM25 retrieval and from flat-file embedding storage to Qdrant represents a meaningful operational improvement beyond raw retrieval metrics. The elimination of separate index-loading steps and the consolidation of document storage, BM25 indexes, and metadata into a single PostgreSQL database simplified data management and reduced the risk of synchronization errors. The key benefit is not raw speed but maintainability: the pipeline is now easier to develop, debug, and extend, which proved critical given the pace of experimentation across four test batches.

The adoption of TEI for embedding generation introduced an API-based workflow that decouples embedding computation from the main pipeline. This architectural choice allows deploying multiple TEI instances in parallel, scaling horizontally to meet the throughput demands of indexing the full PubMed corpus. The API interface also simplifies integration testing, as the embedding service can be developed and benchmarked independently of the retrieval pipeline.

\subsection{Reranker Training and Model Diversity}

Our expanded reranker training pipeline, incorporating 29 models across diverse architectures, revealed several instructive patterns. The LLaMA Nemotron reranker achieved near-perfect validation scores (MAP-bioasq@10 of 0.9995), dramatically outperforming all BERT-based variants. However, validation scores measured on 13B golden data do not necessarily translate to equivalent gains on 14B test sets, where question distributions and annotation patterns may differ. The gap between general-domain and biomedical-specific models narrowed compared to previous years, suggesting that modern large language models pretrained on broad corpora can effectively transfer to specialized biomedical text when fine-tuned with appropriate training data.

The inclusion of dense retrieval results in negative sampling for reranker training was a methodological improvement that we believe contributed to the reranker's ability to discriminate between relevant and irrelevant documents retrieved through different paradigms. This is particularly important in a hybrid retrieval setting where BM25 and dense retrieval may surface different types of false positives.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge versus Agent Quorum}

Although both the LLM-as-a-judge framework and the agent quorum mechanism were available in our pipeline, the agent quorum became the primary approach for most submissions. Several factors drove this preference. First, the agent quorum offers greater flexibility: different agents can be assigned different models, thinking focuses, and document subsets, creating a richer exploration of the evidence space than a single judge evaluating pre-generated answers. Second, the debate process produces an interpretable reasoning trail---each agent's opinion, the evidence it cites, and how its position evolves across rounds---which aids in debugging and qualitative analysis. The LLM judge, by contrast, produces a single evaluation that is harder to audit, particularly when scores diverge from human intuition.

Practical considerations also favored the quorum. The LLM judge requires generating all candidate answers before evaluation, which in a setting with multiple candidate models and configurations becomes expensive in terms of both inference cost and wall-clock time. The quorum's adaptive document retention means agents share the retrieval context incrementally rather than each generating full answers independently. Additionally, the infrastructure for the quorum---batch submission handling, debate state management, and convergence tracking---was more mature at the time of our submissions, having been developed earlier in the pipeline cycle.

That said, the LLM judge remains valuable for exact answer types where correctness is more objectively verifiable against context, and we plan to explore hybrid approaches where the judge serves as a tiebreaker or quality filter on top of quorum-generated answers in future editions.

\subsection{Agent Quorum: Promise and Limitations}

The agent quorum mechanism represents a novel direction for answer generation that moves beyond simple ensembling. By enabling structured debate and iterative consensus-building, the quorum leverages the complementary strengths of different models and thinking focuses. The adaptive document retention protocol addresses a key challenge in multi-agent systems: balancing focus on relevant evidence with exposure to diverse information sources.

A particularly instructive finding emerged from our experiments with different model scales. Across multiple batches, we observed that smaller models (e.g., Gemma~4~E2B and E4B, Nemotron-3-Nano-4B) performed competitively with, and in several cases outperformed, their larger counterparts on most evaluation metrics---with the notable exception of list-type questions. We attribute this to the tendency of smaller models to produce more focused, less over-elaborated representations of the input, which reduces the risk of overfitting to spurious patterns in the retrieved context or overthinking the relationship between documents and the question. Larger models, while more capable of complex reasoning, sometimes introduced unnecessary complexity or tangential information that diluted the precision of the final answer. For list questions, however, the broader context retention capacity of larger models proved advantageous, as these questions require tracking multiple distinct entities across documents.

However, the quorum approach introduces computational overhead proportional to the number of agents and debate rounds. In practice, we found that three to four agents with two to three debate rounds provided a reasonable trade-off between answer quality and inference cost. The convergence criteria proved effective at preventing both premature consensus and endless debate, though we observed that strongly disagreeing agents occasionally produced more insightful final answers than those that reached rapid agreement, as the disagreement forced deeper engagement with the evidence.

The finding that snippets accelerated the quorum process is intuitively appealing: by focusing agents on specific text passages rather than entire abstracts, the debate centers on concrete evidence rather than high-level document summaries. This aligns with the broader observation in retrieval-augmented generation that precise, passage-level context yields more faithful answers.

\subsection{Evaluation Challenges}

Our experience in the 13th edition highlighted the persistent misalignment between automatic evaluation metrics and human judgments, a theme that remains relevant. While the 14th edition's official results provide batch-wise rankings, the true quality of generated answers---particularly ideal answers---can only be assessed through human evaluation. We caution against over-interpreting small differences in ROUGE-based scores, which are known to correlate imperfectly with answer quality in the biomedical domain.

The variability in system rankings across batches, observed both in our results and in previous years, underscores the importance of cross-batch consistency as a design goal. Systems that perform well on one batch may underperform on another due to differences in question types, difficulty distributions, or gold-standard annotation patterns. We view robustness across batches as a more informative signal than peak performance on any single batch.

\subsection{Future Work}

Several directions emerge from this year's work. First, the SPLADE and ColBERT approaches, while not yet deployed in competition, represent promising avenues for improving the retrieval stage. SPLADE's learned sparse representations could combine the efficiency of lexical retrieval with the semantic awareness of dense methods, while ColBERT's late interaction paradigm offers a compelling trade-off between indexing efficiency and retrieval precision.

Second, the agent quorum mechanism can be extended in several ways. Dynamic prompt selection based on question type could optimize agent behavior for different answer formats, and incorporating explicit confidence calibration into the scoring mechanism could improve the reliability of the consensus process. The integration of the LLM judge as a post-quorum quality filter is another promising direction, combining the strengths of both approaches.

Third, we plan to further investigate the relationship between retrieval quality and generation quality. The performance gap between Phase~A+ (system-retrieved documents) and Phase~B (gold-standard documents) indicates that retrieval remains a bottleneck for generation, and continued improvements in Phase~A should yield downstream benefits.

Finally, the snippet generation subtask, while challenging, provides a natural bridge between retrieval and generation. Our fine-tuned Gemma~4 model for snippet extraction showed promise, and improving snippet quality may benefit both the retrieval evaluation (by providing more precise evidence) and the generation process (by supplying the agent quorum with more focused context).
